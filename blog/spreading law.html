Background

This work was one of my graded projects for the computational physics class I enrolled in during my 2nd year. The experimental data comes from an experiment carried out by Kant et al. (2017). The motivation for this work comes from the deposition of micro-droplets in inkjet printing. As droplets fall onto a piece of paper the drop does not stay as a perfect "blob" and spreads out. The behaviour of this spreading can be fully explained by a set of equations known as the Navier-Stoke Equations, however, doing this is extremely complicated. A simpler model is needed! This work analyses the data to figure out which model is better for this scenario. The names of these models are the Cox-Voinov model and the de Gennes model.

What I learned

This project marked my first serious jump into computational physics and Jupyter Notebooks, transforming me from a programming novice to someone capable of implementing complex numerical analysis. I initially struggled to handle large amounts of data into NumPy and creating proper visualizations with Matplotlib, but learning how to use Jupyter's interactive environment allowed me to test each step incrementally.

The biggest challenges came when implementing numerical solutions for contact angles using SciPy's fsolve and fitting spreading laws. It was here I learned the importance of writing clear, concise code that stopped cryptic error messages.

Validating the physical realism of my results was equally critical. While fitting empirical laws such as Cox-Voinov and de Gennes, I questioned whether the derived parameters, like the velocity scale, were physically plausible. Cross-referencing my findings with existing literature helped confirm their validity, reminding me that mathematical models must align with real-world physics. Deciding between competing spreading laws required more than just visual inspection. I relied on statistical tools like χ² analysis and residual plots to determine that the Cox-Voinov law provided a better fit, emphasising the need for quantitative rigor in model selection.

Through persistent debugging and leveraging Jupyter's instant feedback, I not only solved the physics problem but developed core programming skills in data handling, visualization, and numerical methods.
